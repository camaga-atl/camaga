Grammar, Emotions in Thyagaraja's Compositions

 

by

Dr. R. Krishnaswami and

Ram Sriram

 

 

Thyagaraja Swami is a unique combination of a composer, poet, musicologist, philosopher, and saint.  There is no other composer about who so much has been written and spoken, so much analyzed and interpreted and whose compositions are so popular.  A rough estimate shows that over three hundred compositions have been written in his praise. The authors of these compositions about Thyagaraja Swami include his contemporaries such as Sri. Thoomu Narasimha Das, his disciples such as Sri Venkataramana Bhagavathar and Manambuchavadi Venkatasubbier, and composers of a later generation such as Sri Vasudevacharya of Mysore, Sri Ramanathapuram Srinivasa Iyengar, Koteswara Iyer and Sengalipuram Ananta Rama Dikshitar.

 

Fox Strangeways, a German composer and musicologist scholar, in his book the Music of Hindosthan, published in 1914, has devoted much space to praising the greatness of Swami, his compositional techniques and how he has avoided Swaraksharas and other commendations resorted to by less able composers.  Strangeways says that Sri Swami was revered by his contemporaries and that Swami was a person with a clean life and of exemplary discipline.  He lists some eight hundred songs as composed by Swami and adding that perhaps the list is incomplete.  Manali Chinnaswami Mudaliar, Sri Subba Rama Dikshitar's friend and musical collaborator, has published over sixty kirtanas in staff notation and has included complete directions to scales, tala timings, etc. 

 

The "unparalleled" poet

Swami had numerous disciples and this also explains the extreme popularity of his compositions. As Professor S. Ramanathan observed, Swami's compositions were often rendered in identical manner, using the same sangatis by different musicians who learnt and lived in various locations of the country. Professor Ramanathan was surprised to hear the Sankarabharana kriti Mariada Gadura sung in Mysore precisely the same way it was sung in Thanjavur. 

 

Another characterisitc feature of Swami's life was, unlike his, no other composer’s life history was written during his life time.  We have authentic history of the Swami written both by Sri Venkatramana Bhagavathar and his sons, Sri Krishnaswami Bhagavathar.  Research scholars have come up with a wide variety of information about Swami's life and some of this information contradicted common beliefs about Swami's life and work. For example, Sri K. V. Ramachandran has found that Swami lived in affluent circumstances; had abundant time to create his great compositions.  To support his claim, Sri K;. V. Rmachandran says that Swamni owned a home in Melattur and supposedly gifted this home to his Sanskrit preceptor.

 

One more characteristic that differentiates Swami from his contemporaries and others is the fact that Swami is the only composer for whom aradhanas are conducted annually throughout the world. Based on the messages his compositions contain, Swami is praised as Vyasa on vedic matters; Prahlada for his bhakthi; Sukha in his vairagya or determination; Brahma or the Lord Creator himself for his compositions; and Narada for his musical acumen.  He is also referred to as Valmiki, the author of the Ramayana, because of his almost continuous adherence to the Ramayana theme in his compositions. 

 

Musicologist and Nada upasaka

Swami derived much strength for his musical vidwath from his pedigree or family heritage. His grandfathers on both sides were great stalwarts.  Giri Raja Brahmaam, his paternal grandfather, was himself a composer of no mean merit.  At least one scholar says that Gri Raja Brahamaam's compositions are yet available for those interested in singing them. From his maternal grandfather, Swami acquired musical treatises such as Sangita Ratnakara, Naradeyam and Angraha Chudamani.  We would be correct in concluding that it is the study of these great musical works that led Swami to make reference to Bharatha, Kasyapa Someswara and Sarngadeva (see, Vidulaaku Mrokkeda in Mayamalava Gowla).  It is popularly believed that Swami received the Swararnava, the celestial work on music that Siva used to teach music to Parvathi, from Sage Narada himself. Swami references to this in his Sankarabharanam composition, Swara Raga Sudha. 

 

Swami was born during the age of the great musical stalwarts such as Pachimiriam Adiappiah, Veera Bhadrayya and Sonti Venkatasubbiah, father of Swami’s guru, Venkatramaniah.  His contemporaries included the great Shyama Sastry and Dikshitar brothers, Muthuswami, Chinnaswami, and Baluswami.  Marga darsi Sesha Iyengar, Purandara Dasa, Kshetragna, and Bhadrachala Rama Dasa, who preceded Swami, also had left a treasure house full of classical compositions that Swamiji could benefit from.  However, it was not until Swami began to compose that many ragas became popular in classical carnatic music. One of the scholars even goes to the extent of saying that but for Swami’s simple and lilting but strictly classical songs, we would not have any real carnatic classical music left today.  

 

The brief introduction to his compositions and his life point to us that Swami was not a great composer but a great poet and musicologist to who we owe our knowledge of raga lakshanas.
